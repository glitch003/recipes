# Soft Pretzels

Yield: ~8 large pretzels (~75 g each)

## Ingredients

### Dough
- 7 g instant or active dry yeast (1 standard packet, ~2¼ tsp)
- 12 g brown or granulated sugar (1 Tbsp)
- 360 g warm water (~100°F / 38°C)
- 14 g unsalted butter, melted and slightly cooled
- 6 g fine salt (~1 tsp)
- 469–500 g all-purpose flour (spooned & leveled), plus more as needed
- Coarse / pretzel salt, for topping

### Baking soda bath
- 120 g baking soda (½ cup) — see notes for stronger alternatives
- 2.13 L water (9 cups)

### Optional egg wash (for shinier, darker crust)
- 1 egg + 1 Tbsp water, beaten

## Instructions

1. Whisk yeast and sugar into the warm water. Cover and let sit 1 minute.

2. Whisk in melted butter and salt. Add 375 g flour and mix with a wooden spoon or dough hook until combined. Add another 94 g flour. The dough should be slightly tacky and pull away from the sides. If still sticky, add up to 31 g more flour, 1 Tbsp at a time.

3. Knead 3–5 minutes in the mixer or by hand on a lightly floured surface. Add flour 1 tsp at a time only if needed — finished dough should still feel a little soft. Test with the windowpane test: stretch a golfball-size piece thin enough to see light through it without tearing.

4. Shape into a ball, cover lightly, and rest 10–30 minutes. *(For better flavor, rest 60 minutes at room temp, or refrigerate the shaped dough ball for up to 24 hours and bring back to room temp before shaping.)*

5. Preheat oven to 400°F (204°C). Line 2 baking sheets with silicone mats (preferred) or parchment lightly greased with nonstick spray.

6. Cut dough into 75 g pieces (~8 pretzels).

7. Roll each piece into a 50–55 cm (20–22 inch) rope. Form a U, twist the ends twice at the top, then fold the twist down onto the bottom of the loop and press to seal.

8. Bring baking soda and water to a gentle boil in a wide pot. Drop in 1–2 pretzels at a time for 20–30 seconds (no longer — they pick up a metallic taste). Lift with a slotted spatula or spider, drain well, and place on the prepared sheet.

9. *(Optional)* Brush with egg wash for a glossier, darker crust.

10. Sprinkle generously with coarse salt. *(Optional)* Slash the thick belly of each pretzel with a sharp knife — this gives the traditional split-crust look and lets the inside expand cleanly.

11. Bake 12–15 minutes, until deep mahogany brown. Don't pull them early — the color is the flavor.

12. Cool on a rack a few minutes. Best served warm. Optional: spicy nacho cheese sauce, mustard, or melted butter brushed over top.

## Storage

Room temp, covered, up to 3 days. Reheat 5 minutes at 350°F (177°C) or a few seconds in the microwave. Freeze baked pretzels up to 3 months; thaw and reheat at 350°F.

## Notes — about that baking soda bath

The bath is what makes a pretzel taste like a pretzel. It raises the surface pH so the crust browns into that deep mahogany Maillard color and develops the signature tangy, mineral, "pretzel" flavor. Skip it and you have a bread roll shaped like a pretzel.

If you've made pretzels before without it, you probably either used a recipe that called for a baking-soda *spray/dip* instead of a boil, or you made pretzel-shaped bread rolls and didn't realize what was missing.

**Alternatives, weakest to strongest:**

1. **No bath / egg wash only** — soft bread roll. Not really a pretzel. Avoid.
2. **Baking soda spray or quick dip** — dissolve 1 Tbsp baking soda in ½ cup warm water, brush or dip. Faster than a boil, milder flavor, lighter color. Fine for pretzel bites or buns.
3. **Standard baking soda boil (this recipe)** — pH ~8.5. Good color, good flavor, totally safe.
4. **Baked baking soda boil** — spread baking soda on a foil-lined sheet and bake at 250–300°F (120–150°C) for 1 hour. This converts sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO₃) into sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃, washing soda), pH ~11. Use the same 120 g / 2.13 L ratio. Result is noticeably closer to a real Bavarian pretzel — darker, chewier, more flavor — with no special equipment. **This is the upgrade I'd recommend.**
5. **Food-grade lye (NaOH) bath** — 3–4% solution, pH ~13. The authentic German method. Requires nitrile gloves and eye protection while dipping (lye is caustic raw, but neutralizes during baking). Best results, most hassle.
